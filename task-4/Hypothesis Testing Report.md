# Hypothesis Testing Report

# Hypothesis:
Games with higher critic scores tend to achieve higher total sales.

# Objective:
The objective of this analysis is to examine whether critic ratings have a measurable impact on the commercial success of video games. It aims to determine if better-reviewed games perform significantly better in terms of total sales.

# Method:
A Pearson correlation test was performed to evaluate the relationship between critic_score and total_sales. Before applying the test, missing values in both columns were removed to ensure accurate statistical analysis. The Pearson correlation coefficient was used to measure the strength and direction of the relationship, while the p-value was used to determine its statistical significance.

# Result:
The correlation coefficient between critic score and total sales was found to be 0.14, indicating a very weak positive relationship. The p-value obtained was extremely small (less than 0.05), which suggests that the observed relationship is statistically significant and unlikely to have occurred by chance.

# Interpretation:
Although the statistical test confirms the presence of a relationship between critic scores and total sales, the strength of this relationship is very weak. This means that while higher critic scores may have a slight positive effect on sales, the impact is minimal and not strong enough to be considered a key driving factor.

# Conclusion:
The hypothesis is only partially supported. While a statistically significant relationship exists, it is too weak to conclude that higher critic scores meaningfully influence total sales. This indicates that other factors such as marketing strategies, brand popularity, game genre, and platform availability likely play a much more important role in determining the commercial success of video games.
